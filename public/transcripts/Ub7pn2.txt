So let's spend a little bit more time on what chaos engineering actually is. According to the Internet's principlesofchaos.org, chaos engineering is this discipline of experimenting on a system, and we want to build confidence in the capability of that system to withstand turbulence.

A little bit of background about where chaos came from — and I know there are a lot of differences of opinion around the etymology and the history — but what people first started being aware of was from this little media company you may have heard of called Netflix. They had a product, a tool called Chaos Monkey, and what Chaos Monkey's intent was was to — I don't want to use the word randomly — but it would shut off services in production. The intent was to create this need for almost defensive coding, because the reality is you don't know your systems are resilient unless you test them. We're not saying if, we're saying when. The mechanism and the methodology of chaos engineering has matured over the years, so it's not just about shutting things off anymore.

The key word here is that it's about experimenting. Before we talk a little bit more about what chaos engineering is, I want to spend a little time talking about what chaos engineering is not.
