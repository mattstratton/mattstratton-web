If you are not involving your sustainability teams early in the design, you're in for some trouble. If we kind of offload that to the end, they're gonna find some problems and it's too late for them to do anything about it, and it all gets thrown in the backlog and never gets done. See also: security. Understand your indicators, understand those alerting requirements, don't forget documentation. And again, your security stuff needs to happen early. This is why there's a whole new word about this — DevSecOps. Nobody in DevOps ever said security wasn't part of it, but it's okay, we like words.

Maintaining and iterating: we've got our beautiful bouncing baby service out there, it's making money for us. We need to keep it running and keep improving it. Things like remembering to keep that API versioned, communicating to consumers as much as is reasonable. Keeping that proactive maintenance is really key. What do you think about your backlogs? You have to maintain it — you have to feed your dog or else your dog gets really surly and stops playing fetch with you. Continually addressing the tech debt that we discover. It's so very easy to keep pushing that stuff down in the backlog.

And then think about all the things that go into what it means to release this. You need to have a consistent way of understanding what that means.

Sometimes it's time to get rid of services. This is something we really don't think about a lot. This is why you end up with a lot of components and services hanging around that nobody actually knows what they do anymore. People are sometimes very afraid to turn them off. From an old ops perspective, there was always that one box in the data center nobody knew what it did, but damn, nobody was gonna turn it off either. I did have a few change controls when I was at an insurance company where the change was to turn off a server and the validation was: did anybody complain? Those are hard to get past the CAB, by the way. My favorite was when that change got kicked back to say "what's your backup plan" and I was like, turn it back on.

When you're retiring things, identify the consumers of this service as much as you know, and understand what the business impact is. Sometimes there will be impact but it's acceptable. Maybe you've got a couple of customers that are still using that service and they're making you a hundred bucks a month, and your AWS bill for that service is twice that. I'm not an MBA but something tells me maybe it's okay to get rid of that one. Have a process for how you're off-boarding the consumers of this service. If you're shipping them to something different, it's not just "okay, by the way we're turning this off." This is really hard to do well. Service ownership includes communication, compromise, and commitment — it's almost like you're in a relationship with the consumers and customers of your service.
